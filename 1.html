The First Robots

Humans have been wrestling with the idea of making machines which could work independently since ancient times. Ancient myths which speak of artificial people and animals which walk and move like the exactly like the real ones, have been present in all cultures. But beyond legends and myths, there are actual robotic concepts and designs in ancient times which are well documented.A robot is a relatively new term. In ancient days, the term used for machines which could work automatically was ‘Automata’ . A robot is a specialized form of Automata which can do more intricate human-like work. Human-looking robots are now called "androids".
Automata –> Robots –> Androids -> Cyborgs , each different and more efficient than the previous one.

Some automatons work according to prebuilt instructions. Others, more highly developed , are capable of responding to the circumstances around them.Few automata constructed before the sixteenth century have survived, but their existence is recorded.


3000 BC Ancient water clocks: Water clocks were one of the first automata ever devised. It is not known when or where the first water clock was created but one of the first instances of a mechanical device built to regularly carry out a particular physical task occurred around 3000 B.C.: Egyptian water clocks used human figurines to strike the hour bells. The water clocks generally entailed a tank filled with water, but with a small hole in its bottom. So, as water gradually drained through this hole, the water level got reduced – which was equated with the passing of time. Such water clock designs are mentioned in various ancient sources, including Babylonian (17th century BC) Egyptian (15th century BC), Persian (4th century BC), and Indian (3rd century BC).

800 BC: The Greek writer Homer writes about Golden Servants, metal beings that could serve humans. First symbolic mention of robots (automatae) appears in Homer's Iliad - or ‘simulacra’ as they are called later. These Golden Servants were supposed to have been made by the Greek mythological god Hephaestus to serve him and guard the premises when needed.

Hero of Alexandria built a number of mechanical devices which could work freely without human intervention. Of these, the most famous were:
  • A coin operated 'Holy Water Dispenser'.
  • A water organ
  • The Aeolipile, a steam-powered device
  • The Dioptra, a surveying device
